Introduction

The majority of my time spent over the last two quarters in the MEDS program has been spent working on a capstone project investigating the implications of Senate Bill 1137, an important piece of legislation that would impose a 3,200 foot setback distance for which new oil and gas wells would have to be build away from sensitive receptors such as hospitals, schools, and residential areas. This policy has sparked significant debate, as it aims to balance the need for energy production with concerns over public health and environmental protection. Through my research, I have gained a deeper understanding of the complex trade-offs involved in this policy decision, and the difficulties that arise in the renewable energy transition. Overall, this capstone project has been a valuable learning experience, allowing me to apply the knowledge and skills gained in the MEDS program to analyze a pressing environmental policy issue. It has been a deeply rewarding experience, albeit sometimes painful, working on a large workflow that tested my abilities as a data scientist. Lots of debugging and testing had to be completed to even allow the code to run, as the workflow I was updating was passed to us from our clients after having been put together several years ago. Package updates since 2021 and various changes to the data source and structure required extensive troubleshooting and refactoring of the codebase.

In this blog post, I will reflect on my experience and highlight some of the interesting findings from the project. Implementation of machine learning models, creating informative data visuals, and wrangling lots and lots of data have been challenging yet rewarding aspects of this capstone work. I will start by highlight the key results, followed by a deep dive into how I updated the model that was built by the client.

The main addition to the workflow entails a new setback distance of 3,200 feet to reflect SB 1137. The clients created a workflow that estimates the effects of setback distances of 1,000, 2,500, and 5,280 feet on oil production and, subsequently, health, employment, and emissions. Fixing the code up to actually generate this run effectively took much more time than I would like to admit, but connecting the workflow to ensure a clean run in sequential order was imperative to be able to begin estimating the implications of the setback.
